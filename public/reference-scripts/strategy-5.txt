I woke up in a strange world called the Great Chin Dynasty, a place I never learned about in any history class. Before this, I was an outdoor survival streamer, a man who spent his life showing millions of people on the internet how to survive in the wild with nothing but a knife and a tarp. Now, I am Chu Foam, a poor hunter in a snowbound mountain village, with a wife named Zhao Sha Sha and a five-year-old daughter named Meow Meow. Below us, the village of Tawuan is a collection of mud huts and desperate faces. Above us, the mountain is home to a pack of black wolves that have been killing the livestock and the village children. But they do not know that I have my survival gear from the Blue Star, and they do not know that I have already set my traps. I have spent 3 days tracking the pack, using my knowledge of wolf behavior and snow patterns. The wolves are clever, but they are still animals. They move along the same ridge line every evening, looking for easy prey near the sheep pens. I have built a series of deadfall traps along the path, using heavy logs of larch and vines braided for strength. I have also prepared my weapon, a heavy bow of horn and wood topped with bone points I carved with my knife. The wolves are coming tonight. Gara, the leader of the village hunters, thought we should stay inside the walls and wait for spring. He thought we should pray to the stove god for mercy. I told him the stove god doesn't hunt wolves. My wife Zhao Sha Sha watched me build the traps with eyes full of fear, her hands tight around Meow Meow's small shoulders. She didn't believe we could win, but she didn't stop me. She had been hungry for three weeks, and she knew what happened when the wolves finished the sheep. The wolves came at midnight. Seven of them, big, gray, their eyes green in the snowlight, moving like smoke across the clearing. The leader was a massive beast with a white scar across his shoulder, his teeth visible as he sniffed the sheep pen. He took three steps before his paw hit the trigger line of the first deadfall. The larch log fell. It hit his back with a sound like a tree cracking in winter. The wolf went down without a sound. The others froze, their ears flat, their heads turning toward the noise. I pulled my bowstring. I aimed for the second wolf, the one nearest the sheep pen wall. The bone point hit its side cleanly, and it fell, thrashing in the snow. The pack broke. They didn't run away; they charged the hill where I was standing, their claws throwing snow in wide arcs. I had 6 seconds before they reached my position. I threw my bow down. I grabbed my hunting knife, a blade of blue steel I had forged from an old plowshare, and I stepped into the path of the nearest beast. It jumped for my throat. I lowered my shoulder, let its weight hit my chest, and I pushed the blue steel blade into its ribs from underneath. Warm blood covered my hand. The wolf fell, and I stood up, my breath white, my knife red, looking at the remaining four wolves as they stopped in the snow, their heads low, their growling quiet. They looked at their dead leader, then at the child Chu Foam with his knife, and they turned and ran back into the forest. Gara came out of his hut, his spear in his hand, his mouth open as he looked at the three dead wolves in the snow. He didn't speak. He just squatted next to the leader, ran his fingers over the larch log, and gave one slow nod. The village chief of Tawuan villages son came to see me the next morning, carrying a heavy purse of copper coins and three skins of wine. He was a tall man, his coat lined with marten fur, his fingers fat with silver rings. He wanted the wolf skins. He wanted to buy them for 10 copper coins each, a fraction of what they were worth in the city. I told him the skins were not for sale. I told him my family needed the warmth more than his father needed a new collar. He stared at me for a long moment, his smile vanished, his hand on his silver rings, and he said I would regret the day I refused a chief's son. I asked the village chief about my family's history.
