I died. Not dramatically. No last words, no tunnel of light. I was 61 years old, sitting at my desk surrounded by open textbooks on quantum thermodynamics, agricultural soil science, and medieval metallurgy because that was a normal Tuesday for me. My heart simply stopped. One moment I was calculating crop yield coefficients for nitrogen depleted soil. The next, nothing. Then something cold stone beneath me. smoke, animal fat, the smell of wet fur, and unwashed bodies pressed close in a dark space. And above me, two faces, wide jaws, heavy brows, eyes brown and deep, and completely genuinely shocked. Not frightened, shocked. The way you look at something that shouldn't exist, but does. My new parents, I had approximately 10 seconds of confusion before my brain. Intact. every formula, every principle, every accumulated decade of obsessive cross-disciplinary study process the situation. Stone cave, animal skins, bone tools on the ground, fire produced by friction, not flinton steel, no pottery, no woven cloth, hand axes of the isulan type. Paleolithic. I was in the Paleolithic in the body of a newborn. I tried to lift my arm. It flopped sideways like a dead fish. I tried to speak. What came out was a thin, undignified whale that surprised me more than anyone else in the cave. The woman, my mother, pulled me to her chest. Warm. That was something at least. I ran an inventory while she held me. This was a habit from my old life. When facing a new problem, list what you have before deciding what to do. No panicking, no catastrophizing. Data first. What I had the complete accumulated scientific knowledge of human civilization, physics, chemistry, biology, medicine, engineering, metallurgy, agriculture, architecture, linguistics, electrical theory, all of it packed into a brain currently housed in a body that could not support its own head. What I did not have the ability to sit up, speak, hold a tool, or go to the bathroom without assistance. I counted 18 people in the cave. The man, my father, was built like a loadbearing wall. Wide shoulders, scarred forearms, a face broken and healed at least twice. He watched me with his default expression, guarded calculation. A hunter's face. I could tell by the fresh kill hanging near the cave mouth. And by the way, the others deferred to him without thinking about it. I named him Kaido. He moved like still water that was secretly very fast. The old one sitting nearest the fire was different. Finn where everyone else was thick, fewer scars, not from cowardice, but because he had figured out something more valuable than fighting. He watched me with narrow eyes and said something to my father in a low, deliberate tone, filed away, variable to monitor carefully. I named him Goro. Among the others, a stocky man with calloused hands arranged differently from the hunters. The kind of calluses from fine repetitive work, not impact. He turned a piece of flint over in his fingers with unconscious expertise. A craftsman. I named him Tetsu. Promising. A woman with clever eyes and clay dust on her knuckles. Though I saw no finished pottery anywhere. Experimenting then. Good. She was my mother's age. I named her Sora. My mother I named Haru. She was the first warm thing I found in this world. Total 18 people. One newborn with the complete intellectual legacy of homo sapiens. I made my plan while Haru sang to me. Not real music, just rhythmic repetition, soothing in its simplicity. The plan had a structure I'd used my whole life. Work from foundational constraints outward. Don't build a radio before you have copper wire. Don't introduce agriculture before people have a reason to stay in one place. Don't explain germ theory before you have a word for invisible. Step one, language. Not just learning theirs, reshaping it. adding concepts that didn't exist yet. Tomorrow, quantity, plan, cause and effect. Step two, trust. In this world, knowledge came from elders or from spirits. I was neither. I was a baby. Every idea I introduced had to be adopted by the tribe as their own without understanding where it came from. Step three, keep my mouth shut until I had something real to show. The first year of my new life was the most intellectually frustrating experience I had ever endured. I knew 12 ways to synthesize ammonia. I understood the complete theoretical basis for internal combustion. Every object she touched, every sound she made in context, every repeated pairing of word and action, I cataloged it. Their language was more sophisticated than early linguistic models had predicted. These were not primitive grunters. They were people who had built a functional language for their world, a precise one in many ways.
